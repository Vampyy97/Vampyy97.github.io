Volkswagen embodies the challenge ahead for European industry

Editorial Board

Volkswagen, the German automotive giant, has long stood as a symbol of industrial might and engineering prowess. But recent developments underscore the mounting difficulties faced by European manufacturing in the age of global competition, digital transformation and the green transition.

VW’s recent announcement that it would scale back production of its electric vehicles and suspend a number of battery projects in Europe is telling. While demand for EVs is growing, it is not doing so fast enough to meet the group’s original projections. Chinese rivals, meanwhile, are flooding global markets with cheaper, technologically advanced electric models, backed by state subsidies and years of strategic planning.

This exposes a broader issue: Europe’s industrial base is being squeezed from all sides. On the one hand, it must invest massively in new technologies — from EVs to green steel — to meet environmental goals and remain competitive. On the other, it faces higher energy prices, regulatory burdens and sluggish domestic demand.

The US has responded to similar challenges with the Inflation Reduction Act, a massive programme of subsidies and tax breaks aimed at revitalising American manufacturing. China, as noted, is using state capitalism to back national champions. Europe, however, remains divided on how far it should go in offering state support or loosening fiscal rules.

Volkswagen’s predicament illustrates the limits of relying solely on market forces to drive transformation. While competition is vital, it needs to be complemented by targeted public investment, clear industrial strategy and a willingness to support companies through periods of structural change.

Europe’s policymakers must face these realities head on. That means streamlining regulation, investing in infrastructure, improving access to finance for green technologies, and ensuring that the energy transition does not price industry out of global markets.

VW’s difficulties should be a wake-up call. If even its scale and resources are insufficient to navigate the current industrial landscape, smaller players will face even greater hurdles. European industry can still compete globally — but only if it is given the tools and environment to adapt.
