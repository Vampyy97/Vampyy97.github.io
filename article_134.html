Unintended consequences of new online safety rules

Good intentions are laudable, but the internet is rife with unintended consequences. Here are just three examples, all of which undermine Ofcom’s attempts to make the internet safer for children.

Tim Bradshaw’s article highlights the VPN workaround for people wishing to avoid age restrictions (Report, July 28).

The spike in VPN app downloads to which the article refers is a near-real-time reaction to the age verification measures that Ofcom has made mandatory. App downloads are measurable.

A second unintended consequence is that VPNs could lead young people to worse areas of the internet than those they access without a VPN. This is probably unobservable; it’s dark.

A third unintended consequence may take longer to emerge. Ofcom’s guidance lists seven possible ways in which age can be verified. The list is not exhaustive and leaves service providers to determine the technical execution of the checks. Several of the checks involve uploading documents or facial recognition. We should then ask ourselves the next questions in the security sequence: how are these images subsequently processed and stored? In order to demonstrate compliance, might the records be stored for a considerable time?

In digital security, we refer to attack surfaces. The name of the game with security is to minimise the attack surface. Has Ofcom inadvertently expanded the UK population’s attack surface?
