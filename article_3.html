Israel opens Gaza aid corridors after uproar

NERI ZILBER — TEL AVIV
HEBA SALEH — CAIRO

Israel said it had started daily “tactical pauses” to military activity in Gaza and would create “humanitarian corridors” throughout the strip, an apparent reversal of its policy after months of severe aid restrictions pushed the enclave to the brink of famine.

The shift was approved by Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu’s government and announced by the Israel Defense Forces amid growing international condemnation of what UK Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer on Saturday called the “appalling” humanitarian conditions inside Gaza.

The IDF said the halts to military activity, which began yesterday, would happen daily from 10am to 8pm in Gaza City, Deir al-Balah and the Al-Mawasi coastal area, places to which the vast majority of Gaza’s 2.1mn people have been forcibly displaced and where Israeli ground troops are not present.

In addition, the IDF said the humanitarian corridors would be in place from 6am to 11pm every day to enable the “safe passage” of aid convoys.

Tom Fletcher, the UN relief chief, welcomed the announcement and said UN teams on the ground “will do all we can to reach as many starving people as we can in this window”.

The World Food Programme also welcomed Israel’s decision to facilitate access to aid and said it had “enough food in or on its way to the region to feed the entire population ... for almost three months”.

The Egyptian Red Crescent said yesterday that it had sent more than 100 trucks into Gaza carrying more than 1,200 tonnes of flour and 450 tonnes of food packages.

However, one UN official said they were unclear on how long the humanitarian corridors would be in place, while another said aid agencies were in contact with the Israeli military for clarity on details of the new arrangement.

Netanyahu, speaking at an air force base yesterday, said Israel was allowing the entry of “minimal humanitarian supplies”, as it has done “up until now”.

“The UN is creating excuses and lies against the state of Israel,” he said. “There will be no more excuses. We will continue to act until we achieve all of our war goals — until total victory.”

The Israeli air force began parachuting humanitarian supplies into Gaza on Saturday night, while Jordan and the United Arab Emirates also airdropped supplies into the enclave yesterday. UK officials said they were sending additional military planners to Jordan to help co-ordinate the airdrops.

Foreign air forces, including those of the UK, France, Jordan and the UAE, have previously conducted airdrops over Gaza during the conflict. But humanitarian organisations have criticised them as an inadequate means of meeting the territory's needs, with one UN official describing them as a “distraction and a smokescreen”.

David Lammy, the UK’s foreign secretary, said that while the IDF’s announcement was “essential”, it “cannot alleviate the needs of those desperately suffering in Gaza”. “We need a ceasefire that can end the war, for hostages to be released and aid to enter Gaza by land unhindered,” he said.
