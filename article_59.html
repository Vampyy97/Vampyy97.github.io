Houthis vow to step up attacks on vessels using Red Sea

Yemen’s Houthi militants have vowed to “escalate” their attacks on merchant ships, raising the risk for ships on the vital trade route through the Red Sea and the Suez Canal.

The Iran-backed group said in a statement it would attack ships belonging to any company trading with Israel “regardless of the nationality of that company”. The group, which started its attacks in November 2023 and claims to be acting in support of Gaza’s Palestinians, said it was reacting to “rapid developments” in the enclave.

The Houthis in early July sank two ships — the Magic Seas and the Eternity C — in their first Red Sea attacks on merchant shipping since December.

Houthi attacks have sharply reduced the numbers of vessels using the Red Sea and Suez Canal to sail between Asia and Europe, with many shipowners diverting via the longer route round the Cape of Good Hope.

At least 11 members of the Eternity C’s crew are still being held by the Houthis in Yemen, according to one person familiar with the situation.

Martin Kelly, head of advisory for EOS Risk Group, said Sunday’s statement signalled a “shift in intent” from the Houthis. “I think we could see some attacks in the near term,” he said.

Another maritime security expert, speaking on condition of anonymity, pointed out there was widespread international disquiet at the worsening humanitarian crisis in Gaza. “I would have thought, given what is going on in Gaza and the international outrage about it, that would give them cover [for renewed attacks].”

Kelly suggested the statement’s reference to attacking ships linked to any country might indicate a renewed willingness to attack US-linked vessels. The Houthis have been observing an apparent ceasefire with the US in which they avoided attacking American-linked ships while the US held off attacking Houthi assets in Yemen.

The other maritime expert pointed out that the Houthis’ announced targeting criteria were often very loosely applied. When it said it was attacking ships linked to Israel, the UK and the US, it attacked many vessels with only tenuous links to those countries, sometimes on the basis of outdated information.

“In the past, they've... attacked a ship and found a link afterwards,” the expert said.

As well as the crew members being held by the Houthis, 10 seafarers from the Eternity C were rescued by a private security contractor after the attack, according to the person familiar with the situation. Two are confirmed dead, while another two are presumed dead. Only two previous attacks by the group had been fatal, with a total of four seafarers killed in previous incidents.

The person said there were no current negotiations under way between Cosmosphips Management, the Eternity C’s manager, and the Houthis for the seafarers release.
