French seek duty exemption for champagne

ALICE HANCOCK — BRUSSELS
LEILA ABBOUD — PARIS
AIMEE WILLIAMS — WASHINGTON

France is pushing for champagne and other wines and spirits to be exempt from US tariffs as European officials seek to extract carve-outs in a trade deal due to come into force on Friday.

An agreement reached by the EU with US President Donald Trump on Sunday has created confusion in Brussels and Paris over which products will be free from a 15 per cent tariff on most European goods imported by the US.

EU officials insist that talks with Washington are continuing, including on alcoholic beverages. But US officials have said there will be no carve-outs for spirits and wines, including champagne.

French junior trade minister Laurent Saint-Martin has suggested France is close to gaining an exemption for cognac and other spirits. “We understand spirits are indeed exempted,” he said.

But a finance ministry official said they were trying to exclude all alcoholic beverages from the new tariff.

“I can just tell you that we seem to be more advanced on spirits than on wines but we are continuing the engagement here,” an EU official said.

A senior EU diplomat suggested that spirits had secured an exemption, while wine was still being discussed. France is by far the biggest EU exporter of alcohol to countries outside the bloc. In 2024 it exported €12.1bn of alcoholic beverages, according to Eurostat, accounting for 41 per cent of total alcohol exports. About two-thirds of this was wine and the rest spirits, with the largest being cognac. Almost a third of French alcohol exports go to the US.

Its push for carve-outs has been supported by Italy, the EU’s second-biggest exporter of alcoholic drinks, which exported €6bn of alcohol in 2024.

The lack of clarity over the carve-out for European alcohol exemplifies a broader confusion over the specifics of the deal.

Most EU exports to the US will be covered by a 15 per cent standard rate but the commission has said it secured exemptions for aircraft and aircraft parts, certain chemicals and pharmaceuticals, and natural resources.

But factsheets explaining the deal by both the commission and the White House have contradicted each other on issues such as steel tariffs and pharmaceuticals. Neither document mentions beverages.

The commission said the final details would be reflected in a joint statement, likely to be released on Friday, when Trump is also expected to sign executive orders enacting the tariffs.

Both the French government and opposition politicians have criticised the EU-US accord, although Finance Minister Eric Lombard defended it as the best possible outcome in a bad situation.

Wine and spirits groups have warned that additional tariffs could prove critical for the industry. Ignacio Sanchez Recarte, secretary-general of CEEV, the European wine industry group, told the Financial Times that the sector “has been living in great uncertainty” since Trump threatened a 200 per cent duty in March.

The French association of wine and spirits exporters said: “A catastrophe has been avoided but the coming days will be crucial.”
