West risks loss of credibility over Israel, says Norway

Western countries must criticise Israel's actions in Gaza or risk losing credibility when trying to win over the rest of the world on Russia's war in Ukraine, Norway's foreign minister has warned.

Espen Barth Eide told the Financial Times that the west risked being perceived as cherry-picking international law instead of applying principles "consistently" to various global conflicts.

"[By] very correctly criticising Russia, which has broken every rule in the book, but until recently being very quiet on Gaza that tells other parts of the world that we are not really seeing these as absolute norms but more like a menu that we can pick arguments from."

Norway, together with Spain and Ireland, recognised Palestine as a state last year and has sharply criticised Israel's military actions in Gaza as it pushed the enclave to the brink of starvation.

Barth Eide called the conditions in Gaza "worse than hell on earth" at the weekend, before travelling to New York for a meeting at the UN about a two-state solution.

Norway's foreign minister said he kept "reminding my colleagues" that of the 8bn people on the planet, only about 1bn lived in the west. Many in Europe had under-appreciated the linkage that other parts of the world made between Russia's war against Ukraine and Israel's conduct in its war against Hamas.

"We would be well advised to try to understand what they think, and what many out there think is an inconsistent application of international law," he added.

Norway, a founding member of Nato as well as a border country of Russia, is dramatically increasing military spending in response to Moscow's attack on Ukraine. But the Scandinavian country is also an important peace negotiator in global conflicts, including brokering the Oslo Accords between Israel and the Palestine Liberation Organization 30 years ago.

Oslo has faced heavy criticism from the Israeli government for its recognition of Palestine as a state and other comments on the Middle East. Barth Eide said he was aware of all the difficulties of a two-state solution in the region, but that it was "very hard to imagine something better".

Barth Eide added: "We are fully cognisant that a state does not magically appear because you recognise it. That's why we all thought recognition would be at the end of a [peace] process. But we changed our view and [President Emmanuel] Macron changed the French view, [because] there isn't a process. There haven't really been talks since 2013."

Macron has said his country will recognise Palestine in September. British Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer said yesterday that the UK would also recognise a Palestinian state in September unless Israel took immediate steps to end the crisis in Gaza.

The EU has not adopted sanctions against Israel over its actions in Gaza because of disagreements among member states. A review of the association agreement between Israel and the EU in June found that Israel was probably in breach of its human rights obligations.
