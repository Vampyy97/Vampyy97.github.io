Brussels accepts 15% US tariffs to fix ‘unfair trade relations’, says Trump

• EU to buy more US arms and energy • Accord with von der Leyen • Transatlantic strife averted

HENRY FOY — TURNBERRY
AIME WILLIAMS — WASHINGTON
ANDY BOUNDS — BRUSSELS

The US and EU have struck a trade deal that will impose 15 per cent tariffs on EU products, Donald Trump said yesterday.

The EU has agreed to spend hundreds of billions of dollars on additional American energy products and weapons as part of the deal, the US president said.

The agreement, which is set to avoid a transatlantic trade war, was struck during a meeting with European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen at Trump’s Turnberry golf resort in Scotland yesterday.

The meeting, which lasted around an hour, saw the sides agree a deal that was largely hashed out by negotiators earlier last week and met the EU’s key benchmark of a blanket 15 per cent tariff.

Earlier in the day Trump said he wanted to end the bloc’s “unfair” trade relationship with the US. “We should give it a shot,” Trump said ahead of the negotiations.

Sitting next to von der Leyen, Trump said the US-EU trade relationship was “unfair to the United States … a one-sided relationship”. He added: “Both sides want fairness.”

After reaching the deal, he said that “this is probably the biggest deal ever reached in any capacity, trade or beyond trade”.

Trump said the EU would spend an additional $750bn on US energy products, invest $600bn in the US and buy “a vast amount” in US military equipment worth “hundreds of billions of dollars”.

The US president said the US would put tariffs of 15 per cent on EU goods, including automobiles, in exchange for the bloc “opening up their countries at zero tariff”.

Speaking after the US president, von der Leyen said the new tariff rate would be “all inclusive”, and described the arrangement as a “good deal” that had been “tough” to reach.

Before the meeting von der Leyen said that the EU’s trade surplus in goods with the US, which hit almost €200bn last year, needed to be cut.

“We have to rebalance it,” she said.

The deal is a major victory for Trump, who has spent months forcing America’s allies into bruising negotiations by threatening steep tariffs.

But US trading partners have expressed concern that, even when they strike agreements with Trump to lower his tariffs, they face uncertainty over future levies on sectors including pharmaceuticals, chips and aerospace.

EU steel and aluminium imports are exempted from the deal. Trump said the levies, which he has set at 50 per cent for most countries across the world, would not be tackled until after an initial agreement with the EU, dashing the industries’ hopes for an early and low tariff quota.

Trump had threatened to impose 30 per cent tariffs on EU products imported into the US if no deal was finalised by August 1, a move that would almost certainly have sparked retaliation by the bloc and a transatlantic trade war.

The EU had prepared a retaliatory package of tariffs of up to 30 per cent against €92bn of US exports if it could not reach an agreement.

Speaking after the deal was announced, US commerce secretary Howard Lutnick said the US would unveil new tariffs on chips as a result of its national security probe into the sector in two weeks.

TIM BRADSHAW — LONDON

British internet users have rushed to access services that disguise a user’s online location after new age verification rules came into force.

Thousands of sites offering adult content, as well as popular social media apps including X, Reddit and TikTok, have introduced “age assurance” controls for UK users since Friday to comply with the Online Safety Act.

The age checks are designed to prevent children under 18 from accessing sites that carry pornography as well as other harmful material that relates to self-harm, eating disorders or suicide.

But to evade the new rules, a growing number of people are turning to tools more often used by citizens in authoritarian regimes to get around internet censorship.

Apps offering virtual private networks — which route a smartphone or PC’s internet traffic through another country, bypassing local network providers — made up half of the top 10 most popular free apps on the UK’s App Store for iOS this weekend, according to Apple’s rankings.

Proton VPN leapfrogged ChatGPT to become the top free app in the UK, according to Apple’s daily App Store charts, with similar services from developers Super Unlimited and Nord Security also rising over the weekend.

Proton said it had experienced a more than 1,800 per cent increase in daily sign-ups from UK-based users. Nord said there had been a 1,000 per cent increase in UK purchases of VPN subscriptions.

“We would normally associate these large spikes in sign-ups with major civil unrest,” Proton said. “This clearly shows that adults are concerned about the impact universal age verification laws will have on their privacy.”

The UK is one of the first democratic countries to impose such strict content controls on tech companies, making it a test case that will be monitored around the world. Several US states and Australia have also sought to impose age-based restrictions for online services.

Platforms in breach of the rules face penalties of up to £18mn or 10 per cent of global turnover, whichever is greater.

The Act — which was passed under the previous Conservative government in 2023 — has also become a potential flashpoint with the US. Trump administration officials oppose anything they see as a restriction on freedom of speech.

Syria and jet deal test Trump and Erdogan bond

JOHN PAUL RATHBONE — ISTANBUL

It was the body language of an established bromance: Turkey’s President Recep Tayyip Erdogan and US President Donald Trump shook hands, smiled, then clinched each other at a group photo for the Nato summit in The Hague last month.

The “extraordinary” relationship of these two “friends”, as the pair call each other, has so far delivered a string of mutual wins. Turkey has secured a better position than many US allies on tariffs. Washington has lifted sanctions on Syria, as Erdogan wanted. Trump has also turned a blind eye to Erdogan’s crackdown on the opposition.

Erdogan has, meanwhile, provided his fellow Nato ally with a venue for diplomatic talks with Russia, and the possibility of a US military exit from Syria, which Trump has long wanted.

Now, however, their relationship faces two significant tests. The most urgent involves reconciling Turkey’s support for the government of Syrian President Ahmed al-Sharaa with destabilising Israeli military actions in the Arab state. The second is to secure the end of US sanctions on Turkey’s defence sector to allow a multibillion-dollar F-35 fighter jet deal that Erdogan has long wanted.

“You can’t underestimate their chemistry,” Soner Cagaptay, director of the Turkey research programme at The Washington Institute think-tank, said of the two presidents. “Trump likes strong countries and powerful leaders — Erdogan delivers both.”

Even so, “for all the talk of their bromance, Trump and Erdogan don’t actually seem to get any deals done,” said Aaron Stein, president of the Foreign Policy Research Institute in Washington.

Nowhere has the “new era in Turkish-American relations” — as Erdogan has described it — been more evident than in Syria. Turkey’s neighbour had long been a source of tension because of clashing US and Turkish priorities — particularly over US support for Kurdish fighters that Ankara considers an extension of the militant group PKK.

But now Washington appears to all but repeat Turkish talking points — even when they run counter to Israel’s, which has taken a hostile stance towards Sharaa.

In June, after speaking with Erdogan, Trump used his executive powers to lift economic sanctions on Syria, a key Turkish ask. Then the US this month moved to delist as a terrorist group Hayat Tahrir al-Sham, the Syrian president’s former movement — making HTS perhaps the first terror organisation ever to be delisted by the US before it became defunct.

“This was a big deal for Turkey. It was also a sign of Trump’s faith in Erdogan,” Cagaptay said. “If Syria is stable, more of the over 3mn Syrian refugees living in Turkey will go back. That in turn helps with nationalist issues inside Turkey and is a potential vote winner for Erdogan.”

Tom Barrack, one of Trump’s friends and the new US ambassador to Turkey and special envoy to Syria, is a crucial go-between. Trump has tasked Barrack — a smooth talking but tough private equity billionaire of Lebanese descent — with elevating the bilateral relationship from “great” to “extraordinary”.

Barrack criticised Israel for creating “another very confusing chapter” after it launched dozens of air strikes in Syria during a wave of deadly sectarian clashes this month, saying it was acting to protect the Druze religious minority. This rare public censure of Israel from a US official appeared to provoke a tacit rebuttal from Israel’s defence minister Israel Katz: “Those who criticise the attacks are not well versed in the facts.”

The second big test of the Trump-Erdogan relationship is Turkey’s long-held desire to buy dozens of F-35 stealth fighter jets — a deal that Erdogan sounded positive about after meeting Trump at The Hague, saying: “Inshallah, we shall make progress.”

But the obstacles are formidable. The deal was blocked in 2020 under so-called CAATSA sanctions after Erdogan bought two Russian S-400 air defence batteries. Turkish officials have alluded to the S-400s remaining stored in unopened crates ever since. But “if used, it would be like Russia plugging a USB stick into Nato’s computer system,” one western official said. “That is totally unacceptable for us.”

Barrack has suggested that a solution, including the congressional approval required to lift the sanctions, might be found by the end of this year.

In the meantime, Turkey is seeking to recapitalise its ageing air fleet by buying Eurofighter jets. Britain and Turkey signed a preliminary multibillion-pound deal for the sale of Typhoon jets to Ankara last week.

On the F-35s, “the successful formula will require both sides to agree to some concessions to their doctrinal positions,” said Sinan Ulgen, director of EDAM, an Istanbul think-tank. “Turkey will in essence have to commit to never using the S-400 in practice — with monitoring to assuage the trust deficit … But the US will have to show flexibility too.”

The stakes are high. If the issue is resolved, Lockheed Martin could get a multibillion-dollar sales order. Turkish companies could also get reinserted into the F-35 programme, regaining lucrative contracts that once included building F-35 fuselages and serving as one of the jet’s main maintenance hubs.

“It’s going to be a long haul,” cautioned Stein. “To buy the F-35s, Turkey will need military financing — which requires congressional approval. But Turkey is not popular in Congress, and the Greeks and Israeli lobby will oppose the deal.”

Analysts argue that where there is a will there is a way, and expect that the F-35 deal will get done one day — even if that requires a Trump executive order.

Harder to resolve are the growing tensions between Turkey and Israel. Turkish foreign minister Hakan Fidan said last week Ankara might intervene if there were further attempts to divide Syria — a prospect that raises the alarming possibility of clashes between the region’s two most powerful militaries.

Then there is Iran. Turkey bit its tongue when the US bombed Iran’s nuclear facilities in June, issuing only a mild rebuke. But Ankara could fall out completely with the US if there were to be a second round of the Israel-Iran war that dealt more blows to Tehran and which led to a huge influx of Iranian refugees into neighbouring Turkey.

Berlin to avoid EU punishment for breaching budget rules

PAOLA TAMMA — BRUSSELS

Germany will escape punishment for breaching strict EU budget rules, thanks to an exemption that applies to its increase in defence spending, Brussels’ top economic official has said.

Europe's largest economy is forecast to record an excess deficit of 3.3 per cent in 2025, but this is fully accounted for by an increase in defence spending, EU economy commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis told the Financial Times. Berlin, therefore, “is likely not to end up in [the] excessive deficit procedure,” he said.

The EU rules — which Germany helped design — dictate that member states must keep their budget deficit within 3 per cent of GDP and public debt below 60 per cent of GDP. A country could face fines if it does not take corrective measures to meet the rules.

A final assessment of Germany’s position will take place next spring when full data for 2025 is available, Dombrovskis said, adding: “We have to see the execution, because it’s close, [but] if everything holds, then it should not be the case for this year’s budget.”

Berlin’s narrow escape signals a willingness from Brussels to allow the bloc’s largest economy to spend lavishly at a time of higher defence needs and low growth. Germany plans to exceed a 3 per cent annual deficit threshold this year to the end of 2027, according to its own fiscal plan.

But the “national escape clause,” invoked by Germany and 15 other EU countries, allows member states to spend up to 1.5 per cent of their output on defence over four years without breaking the rules.

The clause was added at Germany's behest this year to accommodate higher defence spending, given US demands on EU countries to boost their own security and a new Nato target to spend 5 per cent of GDP on defence by 2035.

The request was a stark about-face from Germany, which during a recent reform of fiscal rules asked for strict safeguards for countries that breached EU debt and deficit thresholds. In negotiations over the past few months with Berlin over its fiscal plan, Brussels granted Germany a right to “backload” measures to reduce its budget deficit towards the later years of its plan.

Brussels has also accepted Germany’s estimate for the potential growth impact of Chancellor Friedrich Merz’s €500bn infrastructure investment fund and increased defence spending at 0.9 per cent on average until 2041 — up from 0.3 per cent in 2025 — something German officials stress is allowed.

Brussels’ leniency towards Berlin is due to its relatively low debt levels, projected to come in only slightly above the EU's 60 per cent threshold at 63.8 per cent of GDP this year.

“The commission has been quite lax in its interpretation of the fiscal rules towards Germany,” said Guntram Wolff, a senior fellow at think-tank Bruegel and a professor of economics at the Free University of Brussels. “At some level, that is understandable given that debt levels are low and also the deficit levels are small compared to France. At another level, it is weakening the just reformed fiscal rules.”

Chile's far right advances as gangs cloud election

CIARA NUGENT — SANTIAGO

A surge in organised crime has reshaped Chile’s politics ahead of November’s presidential poll, handing the country’s far right its best chance at winning power.

Chile, one of Latin America’s safest and wealthiest nations, has suffered its first big wave of gang crime over the past five years, as groups from Venezuela and elsewhere have expanded Chile’s previously niche markets for drugs, human trafficking and extortion.

Kidnappings, dismemberments and other once unheard-of violent crimes have flooded local media and Chile’s murder rate has more than doubled since 2015. Chileans have long ranked security among their top concerns, but polls show the issue eclipses all others.

That has benefited José Antonio Kast, a politician on his third run for president who has made a career criticising governments’ handling of crime and immigration.

Arturo Squella, president of Kast’s Republicano party, told the Financial Times: “Chileans can clearly see that the other competitive candidates have not had the determination to tackle drug traffickers and organised crime head on. They see that determination in José Antonio Kast.”

Kast promises to draw inspiration from El Salvador’s President Nayib Bukele, and build a border barrier to keep out undocumented migrants.

A poll by opinion research group Cadem in July projected Kast to win 27 per cent of the vote, putting him just behind the leftwing coalition’s candidate, Jeannette Jara, at 29 per cent.

But several other rightwingers jointly scored about 25 per cent, and analysts say Kast would more easily pick up those votes in a run-off in December, making the election his to lose.

Kast and Jara have political baggage that may put off moderate voters. Kast, a son of German immigrants and a father of nine, is a defender of Chile’s divisive 20th-century rightwing dictator Augusto Pinochet. Jara is a member of the Communist party, which supports authoritarian regimes in Cuba and North Korea.

“A Pinochetista has never won an election in Chile, but neither has a communist,” said Patricio Navia, a professor at New York University. “This looks like an unprecedented election.”

Crime, along with lacklustre economic growth, has battered the popularity of leftist President Gabriel Boric, a former student leader. He is barred from running for a second consecutive term under Chile’s constitution. Jara was his labour minister.

Boric’s government devoted the first half of his term to failed efforts to expand Chile’s welfare state and overhaul its constitution. A later pivot to focus on security, which he now calls his “number one priority”, was not enough to convince voters and his approval ratings have rarely risen above 30 per cent.

Officials say the arrival of hundreds of thousands of migrants from crisis-stricken Venezuela, combined with a pullback in state presence during the pandemic lockdowns, has allowed poorly policed informal settlements to blossom and created a pool of vulnerable people to exploit, particularly in northern Chile.

Santiago has also changed. Downtown walls are still plastered with graffiti from protests in 2019 over the cost of living. Informal street vendors have proliferated and stores shut earlier.

Fatal shootings have frequently occurred in bustling neighbourhoods around the capital’s Central Station and in July authorities uncovered what appeared to be a “torture house” used by gangs. “Muggers used to just take your cell phone, but now they’ll shoot you... people don’t go out at night any more,” said Marco Gonzalez, who sells bathroom fixtures in the area and plans to vote for Kast. “The government has been too weak to control it.”

Boric’s government has increased security spending, passed laws targeting organised crime and jailed hundreds of gang members, including from Venezuela’s Tren de Aragua gang.

The homicide rate has fallen slightly, from a peak of 6.7 per 100,000 residents in 2022 to six per 100,000 in 2024, and remains among the lowest in Latin America. But other offences remain stubbornly high. A government survey published in July found 5.8 per cent of Chileans had been the victim of a violent crime last year.

“The state has intensely pursued these criminals ... and we are achieving results,” security minister Luis Cordero said. “Of course, I would like the results to come faster, but some will only be visible in the medium or long term.”

Kast argues the government has not gone far enough. He vows to strengthen legal protections for police officers who use force, and make it easier to deport migrants for entering illegally.

China argues for global body to govern AI in challenge to US dominance

WILLIAM LANGLEY — GUANGZHOU
ELEANOR OLCOTT AND RYAN MCMORROW — SHANGHAI

China has unveiled a sweeping plan to expand its role in artificial intelligence governance, including the creation of a global co-operation organisation, as Beijing vies with Washington for technological leadership.

Speaking at the opening of the World Artificial Intelligence Conference in Shanghai on Saturday, Chinese premier Li Qiang said AI innovation was hindered by “bottlenecks” such as the supply of computer chips.

Without explicitly mentioning the US, he took aim at “technological monopolies” and restrictions that could lead to AI becoming “an exclusive game for a few countries and companies”.

“Currently, overall global AI governance is still fragmented. Countries have great differences, particularly in terms of areas such as regulatory concepts [and] institutional rules,” Li said.

“We should strengthen co-ordination to form a global AI governance framework that has broad consensus as soon as possible,” he said, adding that China would help create “a world AI co-operation organisation”.

After Li spoke, the foreign ministry published a 13-point plan for the global governance of AI that proposed the creation of two new AI dialogue mechanisms under the auspices of the UN and a safety governance framework.

In recent months, Beijing has promoted China’s “open” innovation and willingness to “share indigenous technologies”. The country’s top AI groups, DeepSeek and Alibaba, have made their large language models, or LLMs, available to developers worldwide as open-source technology.

China’s plan includes a push for greater co-operation on open-source technology through new transnational platforms and developer communities, and sharing of crucial software and hardware such as semiconductors.

The country’s swath of advanced open-source LLMs has raised concerns in the US that low-cost Chinese tech will undermine Silicon Valley’s global dominance and pricing.

Li’s comments reflect the intense rivalry with the US over the technology, with Washington restricting exports to China of advanced semiconductors and the equipment used to make them, and pressing allies to do the same. China has still managed to make advances, with the release of an LLM by Hangzhou-based DeepSeek this year that sparked a debate about whether the US could maintain its technological edge.

“We are willing to provide more Chinese solutions to the international community and contribute more Chinese wisdom to global artificial intelligence governance,” Li said, adding that AI would be a new engine of economic growth. He said China was especially eager to share technologies with countries in the global south to “make the achievements of AI development better benefit the world”.

China’s foreign ministry said it had invited representatives from more than 40 countries and international organisations to attend the four-day AI conference and exhibition.

China’s plan comes days after the White House unveiled a plan to achieve US dominance in AI. It focused on accelerating innovation by cutting red tape, building infrastructure and ensuring the US led in international AI diplomacy and security.

 

Brazil to increase its Brics commitment in defiance of Trump

MICHAEL STOTT — BRASILIA

Brazil will double down on its commitment to the Brics bloc, the Brazilian president’s top foreign policy adviser has said, a move that defies US President Donald Trump’s threats to impose punitive tariffs.

Trump has taken aim at Brazil twice this month. As the country hosted a summit for the 11-member Brics, he threatened a 10 per cent tariff against any nation “aligning themselves” with the “anti-American” bloc. Trump then pledged 50 per cent levies on Brazil and demanded it end the trial of hard-right former president Jair Bolsonaro, who is charged with plotting a coup.

But Celso Amorim, lead foreign affairs adviser to leftwing President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva, told the Financial Times those attacks “are reinforcing our relations with the Brics, because we want to have diversified relations and not depend on any one country.”

On top of the Brics — which includes China, Russia and India — Amorim said Brazil would look to bolster relations with other countries in Europe, South America and Asia.

The 50 per cent tariff is among the highest mentioned by the US president in his current round of negotiations, despite Brazil running a trade deficit with the US in goods — unlike many other countries targeted. Trump also blasted Lula’s government for what he said are attacks on free speech, a “ridiculous censorship regime” and “very unfair” trade practices.

Lula has rejected Trump's tactics, saying the US president was elected “not to be emperor of the world”. He added that if Trump’s actions during the January 6 Capitol insurrection had been committed in Brazil, he would be standing trial.

Amorim said Trump’s interference in Brazil’s internal affairs was something not seen “even in colonial times”. “I don’t think even the Soviet Union would have done anything like this,” he said, adding that Trump “was trying to act politically inside [Brazil] in favour of his friend.”

While China is the country’s biggest trading partner, importing $94bn of mainly agricultural goods and minerals from Brazil last year, Amorim said “it’s not our intention” for Beijing to be the clear winner from Trump’s higher tariffs.

Amorim denied that the Brics is ideological. He called for the EU to quickly ratify a long-stalled trade treaty with the Mercosur group of South American countries. “If the EU were intelligent, it would ratify it not just for the immediate economic gain but also for more balance in its relations,” Amorim said.

Amorim said Canada was interested in negotiating a free trade deal with Brazil, and added that the last year of Lula’s government was likely to see greater focus on South America.

Remembering the diplomatic maxim that “countries have no friends, only interests,” Amorim said Trump was an unusual case because he had “neither friends nor interests, just wishes”. The US president’s approach is “an illustration of absolute power,” he said.
