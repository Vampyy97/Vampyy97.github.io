Thailand and Cambodia agree pact to end fighting

Thailand and Cambodia agreed a ceasefire to end fighting along their shared border that has killed more than 30 people and displaced about 300,000 in recent days.

The pact, due to come into effect last night, was sealed by leaders of the two neighbouring south-east Asian nations at talks in Malaysia held as their governments traded accusations of firing into each other's territory for a fifth day. US President Donald Trump had warned he would terminate trade talks with both nations if they did not stop fighting.

Anwar Ibrahim, Malaysia’s prime minister and rotating chair of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations, unveiled the pact in Kuala Lumpur, flanked by the Cambodian and Thai leaders. “Prime Minister Hun Manet and acting Prime Minister Phumtham Wechayachai have expressed their positions and willingness for immediate ceasefire, and [a] return to normalcy,” Anwar told a news briefing. The Malaysian prime minister added Trump had “been in contact” with both countries.

He said Thailand and Cambodia had agreed to a meeting of their regional military leaders today, to be followed by a meeting of defence attaches.

They would also convene a gathering of the General Border Committee, a bilateral military group, on August 4.

In a post on his Truth Social platform, Trump claimed credit for the ceasefire and said he had called the Thai and Cambodian leaders. “I have instructed my Trade Team to restart negotiations on Trade,” Trump wrote. “I have now ended many Wars in just six months — I am proud to be the President of PEACE!”

The Malaysian leader said China, which has friendly relations with both Cambodia and Thailand, had kept in “close contact” with them and Malaysia during efforts to broker a ceasefire.

China’s foreign ministry said yesterday it was “deeply saddened” by the casualties caused and that it intended to “play a constructive role in promoting a ceasefire and ending the conflict”.

Hun Manet said he hoped to stop immediately the fighting, which had “cost many lives” and created 140,000 refugees in Cambodia and 160,000 in Thailand. Phumtham thanked Anwar, Trump and China for helping to broker an outcome that reflected “Thailand’s desire for a peaceful resolution”.

Fighting broke out last week amid rising tensions between Thailand, a US treaty ally, and Cambodia over a long running and emotive border dispute. The two sides accused each other of targeting civilian areas.

Earlier yesterday, they continued to exchange fire. Cambodia claimed Thailand had fired first after 3am on Monday. Thailand said it had been attacked, and fired back to defend itself and prevent an “incursion into strategic terrain”. It dismissed Cambodia’s claim its forces had used chemical weapons as “groundless”.

“Cambodia had very little option but to secure a ceasefire,” said Virak Ou, founder of Future Forum, a Phnom Penh-based think-tank. “The US is the biggest market for Cambodian exports, so any trade deal with the US will have broad implications for the economy for years to come.”
