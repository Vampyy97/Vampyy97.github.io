Union Pacific and Norfolk Southern agree $250bn combination

OLIVER BARNES — NEW YORK

US railroad operator Union Pacific has agreed to acquire Norfolk Southern in the biggest deal of the year, which will create a transcontinental rail juggernaut valued at $250bn.

The largely stock-based tie-up, which would forge the largest railroad operator in the country with more than 50,000 miles of track across 43 states, comes as the industry grapples with weaker freight volumes as well as high fuel and labour costs.

A combination of Union Pacific, which operates west of the Mississippi River, and Norfolk Southern, in the east, would be the first operator in US history capable of carrying goods from the Pacific coast to the Atlantic on its own tracks. It would also help to ease a logjam around the main interchange point in Chicago.

The deal, announced yesterday, is likely to face intense scrutiny from regulators. The industry’s last significant tie-up, Canadian Pacific’s $31bn takeover of Kansas City Southern, took about two years to secure regulatory approval.

The fate of the deal will probably hinge on whether it can be pitched to President Donald Trump’s administration as a boon for US infrastructure and way to cut clients’ costs.

‘Railroads have been an integral part of building America since the Industrial Revolution.’

The deal values Norfolk Southern at $320 a share, giving the group an $85bn enterprise value. Union Pacific has an enterprise value of about $165bn, making the combined group worth about $250bn.

“Railroads have been an integral part of building America since the Industrial Revolution, and this transaction is the next step in advancing the industry,” said Jim Vena, Union Pacific’s chief executive.

About a quarter of the acquisition will be paid for in cash and the remainder in stock. Norfolk Southern shareholders will receive one Union Pacific share and $88.82 in cash for each share of Norfolk Southern, the companies said.

The Republican-controlled Surface Transportation Board, the industry regulator, will have to approve the deal, and interested parties, including the justice department, labour unions and rail operators such as Amtrak, will be given the opportunity to raise any objections.

If it were to secure regulatory approval, the acquisition would be the biggest since Microsoft’s $75.4bn takeover of Activision Blizzard, which closed in 2023. Similar to the Activision Blizzard takeover and Nippon Steel’s acquisition of US Steel, the railway merger will face a long, drawn-out path to closing.

The deal is structured without a voting trust, a mechanism that in past US rail mergers has allowed shareholders in a target company to sell up and avoid the risks of the STB’s approval process. If the agreement does fall apart, Union Pacific will have to pay Norfolk Southern a $2.5bn reverse termination fee.

Mark George, Norfolk Southern’s chief executive, said the deal would contribute “to igniting rail’s ability to deliver for the whole American economy today and into the future”.
