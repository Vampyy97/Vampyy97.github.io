Fintechs raise the alarm over plan by JPMorgan to charge for its data

Start-ups say bank's move to end free access to customer information is a thinly disguised ploy to kill competition
AKILA QUINIO AND JOSHUA FRANKLIN
NEW YORK

JPMorgan Chase has alarmed financial technology start-ups with plans to charge them to access its customer data, a move that could endanger their business models and is seen by some as chief executive Jamie Dimon's attempt to crush nascent competitors.

At the end of June, the largest US bank by assets sent out price sheets to start-ups that have long relied on free access to bank consumer data to power other financial services. This includes data aggregators, such as Plaid, that have grown in the past decade thanks to this free data-sharing model.

The dispute highlights the interconnected nature of banks and non-bank companies in providing financial services in a digital world, and the cost of sharing the data between the two sides. But the proposed charges by JPMorgan have triggered outrage from smaller fintech players.

Many of them argue that their business models would not be able to withstand the steep fees and that those that will would be forced to pass down significant costs to consumers.

Steve Boms, executive director of the Financial Data and Technology Association, a trade group representing about 30 aggregators and fintech companies, said that JPMorgan's action was a "pure and simple" attempt to kill competition that would "put third parties out of business altogether".

"Across all the companies that received the notices, the cost of just accessing Chase data is somewhere from 60 percent and in some cases well over 100 percent of their annual revenue for the year," said Boms, whose group's members include Plaid, Fiserv and Intuit. "Just from one bank."

Chase's fees could take effect as early as the start of September, though the bank is in talks with the various companies that could alter the timeline and the amounts charged.

"We have explicitly reserved the right to charge a fee for data access since day one of our agreements," a JPMorgan spokesperson said.

Under rules finalised by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau during former US president Joe Biden's administration, banks would be prohibited from charging third parties for access to consumer data. However, the gutting of the regulator by the Trump administration has led to this rule being changed, opening the door for banks to set their own fees.

JPMorgan's argument is that banks have spent millions of dollars to build APIs, or application programming interfaces, that allow fintechs safely to access customer data. The bank says that of the 2bn monthly API requests it receives from aggregators, about 90 per cent are unrelated to a customer using a fintech service.

"Having a charging structure will ensure that data is provided only when customers request it, and that data middlemen are fostering a safe, secure data ecosystem that we built and maintain—and that their entire industry was built on," a bank spokesperson said.

Inside the bank, there is a view that these costs and requests will only significantly increase in the years ahead. The Bank Policy Institute, an industry lobby group, argued earlier this month that "data sharing comes at a cost".

"Charging data middlemen responsible for the largest volume of requests allows banks to cover basic business expenses without passing those costs on to consumers," the BPI said.

JPMorgan's Dimon has also consistently warned of the threat of fintechs to banks. In April, the CEO wrote in his annual letter that his bank needed "to be as nimble as they are and use our skills and capabilities to stay ahead".

Other banks could follow JPMorgan's lead—Pennsylvania-based PNC, the ninth-largest US lender by assets, has said it is discussing it.

"I applaud what JP did. I think they're exactly right," PNC chief executive Bill Demchak told analysts earlier this month.

The Financial Technology Association, which represents the fintechs, has taken its case to the courts calling on them to uphold the CFPB's revoked rule, which it said "reflects a bipartisan commitment to modernising how Americans manage their financial lives".

"We've seen the nation's biggest banks really take advantage of this moment of regulatory uncertainty to consolidate their position in the market and to crush competition," said Miranda Margowsky, an FTA spokesperson.

Critics of the fee-charging model also note that the move would make the US an outlier in explicitly allowing banks to charge for open banking access.

The data-sharing technology has flourished in jurisdictions where it is mandated to be largely free, including the UK, Brazil and Sweden.

"It would put us completely the opposite of every other country with open banking," said Boms.

Fintechs are also seeking to gain political traction by highlighting the risk that the move could adversely affect the crypto industry—an area championed by the Trump administration—because crypto companies also rely on open banking access to move money between bank accounts and crypto wallets.

In a letter to US President Donald Trump, trade groups that included the Blockchain Association and the Crypto Council for Innovation urged him to intervene and reverse the regulator's decision.

Trump's son Donald Trump Jr and David Sacks, an influential crypto adviser to the White House, have publicly sided with the fintechs against JPMorgan on social media platform X.
