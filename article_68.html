ECB warns over threat to monetary policy from Stablecoins

Olaf Storbeck — Frankfurt

The rise of stablecoins linked to the US dollar threatens to undermine the European Central Bank’s control over monetary policy, a senior official from the central bank has warned.

Jurgen Schaaf, an adviser in the ECB’s market infrastructure and payments unit, warned that the rapid adoption of stablecoins could leave the Eurozone facing similar conditions to “dollarised” emerging economies, where widespread use of the US currency can hamper local policymakers' efforts to set interest rates or control the money supply.

Global circulation of stablecoins — tokens that track the value of fiat currencies and are a cornerstone of crypto trading — has risen to about $250bn. The vast majority are linked to the dollar as President Donald Trump pushes to establish them as a pillar of mainstream finance.

“Should US dollar stablecoins become widely used in the euro area — whether for payments, savings or settlement — the ECB’s control over monetary conditions could be weakened,” wrote Schaaf in an article published on the ECB’s blog yesterday.

He added that a “disorderly collapse” of private sector stablecoins could “reverberate across the financial system” with central banks being increasingly concerned about contagion.

Tether and Circle operate the two largest stablecoins but the passage this month of landmark US legislation to regulate the sector is expected to pave the way for Wall Street banks to launch their own offerings.

Stablecoins are backed one-for-one by safe assets such as government bonds and are designed to act as a bridge between cryptocurrencies and the mainstream financial system.

Their backers argue that they are more efficient than international bank transfers but their anonymity has made them popular conduits for drug trafficking and money laundering.

The ECB’s warning marks the latest concern among central bankers over the growth of stablecoins.

The Bank for International Settlements said last month that stablecoins “perform badly” as money because they are not backed by central banks, lack sufficient guardrails against illicit usage and do not have the flexibility of funding needed to generate loans.

Trump’s drive to promote stablecoins would underpin the greenback’s global dominance and could lower US borrowing costs, according to Schaaf.

“For Europe, this would mean higher financing costs relative to the United States, reduced monetary policy autonomy and geopolitical dependency,” he wrote.
