US freezes export curbs in push for China accord

The United States has temporarily frozen restrictions on technology exports to China to avoid hurting ongoing trade talks with Beijing and to help President Donald Trump secure a meeting with President Xi Jinping this year, according to people familiar with the matter.

The Commerce Department’s Bureau of Industry and Security, which runs export controls, has been asked to hold off on tough moves on China, according to multiple current and former US officials. US and Chinese officials met in Stockholm yesterday for a third round of trade talks, following previous meetings in Geneva and London.

While Trump wants to avoid actions that could disrupt efforts to meet Xi, some officials argue that the US is hamstrung on export controls because of the risk that China retaliates by restricting the export of rare earths and magnets. China had already imposed such curbs in May for the first time.

Earlier this year, Trump’s team prepared to restrict technology exports to China. In April, they notified Nvidia that it would block the export of its H20 chip, which was designed for the Chinese market after the Biden administration had already restricted more advanced chips. However, Trump reversed course following direct lobbying from Nvidia chief executive Jensen Huang.

The H20 has become the focus of a power struggle between security officials, who say the chip will help China’s military, and Nvidia, which argues that blocking US technology exports will simply push Chinese groups to accelerate innovation.

Twenty national security experts and former officials—including Matt Pottinger, deputy national security adviser in Trump’s first administration—were understood to be writing to Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick to express concern. The letter, seen by the Financial Times, was also signed by David Feith, former top National Security Council technology official in the current Trump administration, and Liza Tobin, who served in the NSC during Trump’s first term. Kyle Bass, a Trump supporter and founder of Hayman Capital Management, also signed.

The experts argue that the H20 is a “potent accelerator of China’s frontier AI capabilities,” outpacing the advanced Nvidia H100 chip (which is blocked for export) in “inference”—the execution of AI functions as opposed to the training of AI models. They warn that it would help China develop autonomous weapon systems, intelligence surveillance platforms, and other military advancements.

Nvidia said the criticisms in the letter were “misguided” and “inconsistent” with the AI action plan recently unveiled by Trump.
