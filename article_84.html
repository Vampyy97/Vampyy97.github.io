China's textbook approach to the tariff talks with the US

Beijing has subtly transformed the impending August 12 deadline to meet a durable tariff agreement with the Trump administration (“China at the negotiating table”, Unhedged, FT.com, July 23) from a hard cut-off into a negotiable pause, opting to offer a tariff “freeze” rather than a full rollback.

By framing their proposal as a temporary suspension, China retains room to manoeuvre domestically, where monetary and fiscal tools are already stretched, and maintains the narrative of strength at home.

At the same time, Chinese envoys signalled modest capacity reductions in steel and other industries as symbolic concessions, a tactic that reinforces mutual obligation without ceding the appearance of control.

This careful orchestration echoes the principle of guanxi: each small gesture deepens interdependence, keeps US negotiators engaged and allows Beijing to dictate the pace of talks rather than simply react to Washington’s timetable.

Beyond these surface moves, China has quietly shifted attention to critical supply chains, reminding its counterpart that disruption of rare earth magnet exports (where it supplies about 90 per cent of the world market) would have immediate, tangible effects on US manufacturing.

By highlighting this less visible pressure point, Beijing injects time sensitivity into the dialogue and reframes the discussion around securing essential inputs, not just tariff levels.

For negotiation specialists, it’s a textbook demonstration of creating visible tariff concessions buy goodwill and headlines, while strategic control of vital materials ensures deeper stakes remain on the table.

In doing so, Chinese negotiators preserve the option to tighten or loosen pressure in response to US proposals, keeping themselves firmly in the driver’s seat of a more balanced, multi-dimensional negotiation.
