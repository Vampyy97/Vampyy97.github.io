Clubs aim to build on Euro 2025 momentum

England’s penalty shootout victory over Spain in the Euro 2025 final on Sunday thrilled fans of the Lionesses but the longer-term prize could be a surge of investor interest in women’s football leagues and clubs.

Club officials, advisers and investors told the Financial Times that the women’s tournament, which featured record prize money of €41mn as well as record attendances, could win more fans, and attract brands and ultimately investors to the women’s game.

“Euro 2025 has demonstrated that women's football has cross-border appeal, not just a local or national following,” said Ebru Koksal, chair of campaign group Women in Football and adviser to women’s sports-focused investment firm Mercury13.

The tournament attracted a record attendance of more than 657,000 fans across the group stages and knockout ties. The stakes are high since Europe’s domestic leagues and clubs will aim to carry the momentum into the new season, with investors watching closely.

If new fans transfer to the domestic game, brands and broadcasters should follow, in theory boosting investor demand and valuations, said Andrew Umbers, co-founder of London-based sports advisory Oakwell.

The Euros final attracted a peak audience of more than 16mn viewers on BBC and ITV, a statistic that “very publicly” demonstrated the size of the audience of women’s football in England, he said.

Umbers thinks Euro 2025, organised by Uefa, will boost investor appetite for “top-level” women’s football clubs. In particular, he expects “the floodgates to open with respect to investment” into clubs playing in the Women’s Super League, the highest level of the game in England.

However, Jennifer Haskel, a sports expert at consultancy Deloitte, cautioned that the Lionesses’ success “doesn't necessarily mean that everything’s going to be picture perfect from now on”.

“We still need to continue to build and continue to showcase growth from ... new fan engagement, new ticket strategies, new commercial strategies that are specific to the women’s game,” she said.

More investment would follow what has been a flurry of high-profile deals in the women’s game, including the $250mn takeover of Los Angeles-based Angel City FC, which plays in the US National Women’s Soccer League, by Bob Iger and Willow Bay in 2024.

Advisers and club executives pointed to efforts by English clubs to attract external investment to their women’s teams.

Alexis Ohanian, co-founder of social media platform Reddit, bought a roughly 8 per cent stake in the Chelsea women’s team for about £20mn in May, valuing the WSL champions at around £250mn. Everton and Aston Villa are also exploring the possibility of bringing external investors into their women’s teams, according to people with knowledge of the matter. Chelsea, Everton and Aston Villa declined to comment.

Sarah Batters, managing director of WSL team London City Lionesses, says that, in light of these developments, the national team’s triumph is a “boost at the right moment”.

“There’s probably more reason for hope of an upward trajectory and not just a flash in a pan,” she said.

Michele Kang, owner of London City Lionesses since December 2023, is betting more widely on women’s football. She has also taken over the Washington Spirit in North America’s NWSL and French team OL Lyonnes, and has led a $2mn investment in women’s football boot specialist IDA Sports.

Kang’s Kynisca Sports International is among the new breed of investors targeting women’s football. Mercury13, which bought Italy’s FC Como Women last year, is seeking more investments in the women’s game. Monarch Collective, which also focuses on women’s sports, launched in 2023 and is an investor in Angel City FC among other NWSL teams.

However, one sports banker, who preferred to remain anonymous, cautioned that women’s football clubs, particularly in Europe, faced an uphill battle to be profitable.

He said that a wider risk in European football, including the men’s game, was that many clubs relied on owners to inject capital.

The 12 clubs in the WSL reported an aggregate pre-tax loss of £28mn in 2023-24, according to Deloitte. Revenues rose 34 per cent to £65mn in the 2023-24 season, while wages increased 44 per cent to £52mn.

Experts say it will take time for revenues to catch up with the valuations being negotiated with investors. Koksal believes the test is whether fans who enjoyed moments of national pride at the Euros continue watching the club game at stadiums and on media platforms.

“This will lift club valuations and expand investor interest well beyond the UK,” Koksal said.
