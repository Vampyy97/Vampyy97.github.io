Here’s why US capacity to supply Ukraine isn’t infinite

The editorial “Trump’s overdue pivot on Ukraine” (FT View, July 21) captures the excitement in Europe at the opportunity to leverage President Donald Trump’s apparent shift towards supplying Ukraine with more US munitions. But expectations on both sides of the Atlantic should be tempered by reality.

In a single recent Russian strike on July 9, Ukraine was attacked with 728 Shahed-style drones, seven cruise missiles, and six ballistic missiles. The US currently produces just about 600 Patriot interceptor missiles per year — fewer than were needed to defend against that single attack if the system was relied on exclusively. There is no surge capacity left. Expanding production isn’t a matter of flipping a switch; it’s a years- or even decades-long process involving labour shortages, strained supply chains, and complex component bottlenecks.

Worse, Ukraine is only one of the regions clamouring for these munitions. US forces require interceptors for their own operational needs in the Middle East and Asia, where the threat from Iranian and Chinese missile stockpiles remains a significant challenge. At a certain point, transfers will stop — not because of politics, but because there simply won’t be any missiles left to send regardless of who’s paying for them.

Ukraine is developing its own drone interceptors and receiving other systems from allies, which helps alleviate over-reliance on limited US supplies. But these can’t compensate for the scale and reliability of US platforms.

If Europe wants to chart a long-term path to stability, it must reckon with these hard limits. America is overextended in an increasingly unstable world, and its capacity to support this war is not infinite.

Robert Clarke
Fredericksburg, VA, US
