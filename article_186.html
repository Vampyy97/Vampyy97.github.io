How to mitigate future financial meltdowns

Sheila Bair’s plea (On Wall Street, July 19) that regulators should continue to maintain supplementary leverage ratios at “global systemically important banks” is well founded. These too-big-to-fail banks will play an increasingly important role in these rapidly changing economic times where, for example, $1tn or more could be withdrawn from banks and other investments and used to buy unproductive bitcoins.

However another, more basic, change — requiring lenders to sell all loans “with recourse” — could drastically reduce risk and would revolutionise the lending industry at very low cost to regulators. Any loan that went sour would have to be repurchased by the seller of that loan. Alternatively they could substitute a comparable “performing” loan.

This approach would ensure that each lender, including both the originator and the packager of loans, acted prudently. It could prevent or at least mitigate future meltdowns.

Michael Westgate Former Assistant to the Director, FDLIC (1971-75), Chatham, MA, US
