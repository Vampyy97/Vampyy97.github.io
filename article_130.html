The science of starvation

In the 1940s, as war spread in Europe and beyond, an American physiologist realised that starvation would surely follow but that little was known scientifically about its effects. Ancel Keys, a Minnesota University professor and consultant to the US War Department, seized the chance to fill in the gaps.

In 1944, together with the psychologist Josef Brozek, he recruited 36 healthy male volunteers willing to be observed, measured, questioned and assessed as they were first starved and then fed back to health. The Minnesota Starvation Study became a landmark investigation into how extreme food deprivation affects the human body, mind and spirit.

Along with accounts of such atrocities as the Bengal Famine and the Holocaust, the study has informed a science of starvation that remains hauntingly relevant today. A UN-backed body that tracks food security said yesterday that “widespread starvation, malnutrition and disease” are driving hunger-related deaths in Gaza, with the territory on the brink of famine. More than 20,000 children have been treated for acute malnutrition and at least 16 have died.

Keys’ volunteers, all conscientious objectors, responded to a wartime appeal entitled: “Will You Starve That They Be Better-Fed?” For the first three months, the men, who lived in Keys’ lab, ate about 3,200 calories a day. They had to walk 22 miles a week and keep a journal. Throughout, Keys measured their weight, size and strength; there were X-rays, treadmill sessions and blood samples, as well as intelligence and personality tests.

For the next six months, calorie intake was roughly halved and comprised two daily meals of simple foods like toast and potatoes. The men lost around a quarter of their body weight and 40 per cent of muscle mass; they became weak, argumentative and introverted. They suffered dizziness, swollen ankles, anaemia, hair loss, low libido, skin changes and ringing in the ears; they felt cold, even in summer, and dropped out of classes.

Heart rate and blood pressure fell, challenging the idea these were largely fixed characteristics. They became obsessed with food (after the experiment, Keys reported, three became chefs). Thirty-two of the 36 men lasted the distance.

The last three months focused on replenishing calories; the volunteers admitted to finding this as challenging as being semi-starved. They found it hard to regulate appetite, with several eating until they vomited; many struggled with overeating after returning to normal life. Keys and co-authors gathered their findings in The Biology of Human Starvation, published in 1950 and still cited today for its relevance to eating disorders.

When interviewed two decades ago, the 18 then-surviving Minnesota volunteers insisted they had no regrets about their ordeal and did not think it had harmed them.

The problems Keys encountered in treating starvation were echoed in a 1951 study of liberated Japanese prisoners of war; a fifth died after being fed. This phenomenon, also seen in Holocaust survivors, is known as “refeeding syndrome”: a sudden influx of nutrients can trigger a potentially fatal imbalance of fluids and electrolytes in a long-starved body.

Many starving people consume much less than half the calories they need. When the glucose from regular meals runs out, a famished body will eventually turn on itself, scavenging fat and then protein, including muscle. While we are evolutionarily adapted to periods of hunger, prolonged starvation weakens the tissues, such as heart muscle, that keep a person alive, and increases vulnerability to disease.

Studies suggest an adult can survive without eating for around two months as long as they are drinking water. Starvation is more catastrophic for children, especially in the first 1,000 days of life, because it hampers growth, brain development and immunity. Famine damages future generations in the womb: children born to starving mothers are more likely to develop obesity and diabetes.

Keys, who died in 2004 aged 100 and became best-known for designing army rations and linking cholesterol to heart disease, also made wider observations that resonate with events today. A paper in the Journal of Nutrition notes he “stressed the dramatic effect that starvation had on mental attitude and personality, and argued that democracy and nation building would not be possible in a population that did not have access to sufficient food.”

As Keys saw hunger turn healthy men into living ghosts lacking hope, he came to understand that starvation was a political issue, as well as a scientific one.
