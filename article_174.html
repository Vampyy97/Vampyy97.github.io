Iron ore woes send profits at Rio Tinto to five-year low

Rio Tinto has recorded its weakest first half profit for five years after a slump in the iron ore price and cyclones in Western Australia hit its biggest source of income.

The Anglo-Australian miner yesterday said results for the six months to June were “very resilient” given a 13 per cent drop in the average iron ore price during the period. The decline hit group earnings before interest, tax, depreciation and amortisation by about $2bn, chief financial officer Peter Cunningham told the Financial Times.

Rio’s performance in copper, a key growth metal for the mining industry, and aluminium made a key contribution. “We're getting so much more contribution from aluminium and copper that you can see we’re less dependent on the iron ore price,” said outgoing chief executive Jakob Stausholm.

The results were the last to be presented by Stausholm, who is being replaced next month by Simon Trott, head of iron ore. Trott is expected to drive a group restructuring.

Stausholm said the miner had “solid foundations in place”, having invested in growth markets, including lithium, during his tenure. The outgoing chief was appointed five years ago after Rio destroyed a sacred indigenous site at Juukan Gorge that led to the exit of its former chair and chief executive. Stausholm yesterday called it “a horrible event” but said it had led to a renewed emphasis on improving the culture at the company.

Rio's underlying ebitda dropped 5 per cent to $11.5bn in the six months while underlying earnings fell 16 per cent to $4.8bn — the lowest level since 2020. Rio has proposed a dividend of $1.48 a share, down from $1.77 a year ago and below analyst expectations.

Stausholm said iron ore remained the “crown jewel” of its operations but the company would seek ways to save costs. “The magic words at Rio Tinto are continuous improvement,” he said.

President Donald Trump's tariffs on copper, aluminium and steel had created a turbulent environment for miners. Stausholm said they had become a “huge topic” for the industry but that Rio had been able to navigate their impact on its aluminium production in Canada while it should benefit from tariffs on copper given that it had some production in the US.

Meanwhile, trading house Glencore was hit by a 26 per cent slide in copper production during the first half of the year and said it aimed to make cost savings of about $1bn by the end of 2026. Glencore will report earnings next week.
